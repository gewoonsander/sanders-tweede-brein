# Too much input is a filing problem, not a willpower problem.

In today's video, we are tackling information overload. One of the biggest challenges we all face in this digital age. Let's dive into the solution that has helped me and many others regain control. We live in a world where we are constantly bombarded with information. Articles, podcasts, videos, emails, you name it. It feels like every day we are handed more and more data to process. This leads to what I call the information paralysis where we gather so much but can't take meaningful action because we are overwhelmed by sheer volume. Most note-taking and personal knowledge management systems don't address this issue at the root. They focus on storage but neglect how we can effectively process and utilize this information. Traditional methods like saving everything into a big pile of folders or relying solely on tags can either lead to chaos or an inability to retrieve the information we actually need the moment we need it. It's not just about saving information, but managing and retrieving it in a meaningful way that can drive action and productivity. So, how do we tackle this? In my productivity journey, I've discovered that managing information from two key sources. The outer world and the inner world is the secret. The outer world is everything external. Articles, podcasts, social media posts, books, and videos. The inner world is where your thoughts, insights, and reflections come into play. Integrating both worlds into a seamless system is crucial for effective note-taking and knowledge management. Step one is to capture information efficiently. Whether it's a thought during a meeting or an interesting article you found online, having a reliable method to capture that information is essential. The mistake many people make is trying to organize everything right away. Don't just capture. Once you've captured information, it's time to organize. Now, instead of dumping everything into folders, we can use a hybrid system of folders and tags. Here's how it works. Folders allow you to categorize information in broad categories. Think of them as containers for specific projects or topics. Tags, on the other hand, help connect ideas across different folders, ensuring nothing gets lost in isolation. Let's say you're researching productivity tips. You might have notes spread across various projects. But by tagging each node with productivity, you can retrieve everything in one search. This interconnected approach ensures you don't lose sight of valuable information. Here's the game changer. Not everything needs to be connected. It's crucial to know when to isolate information. Sometimes simplicity is the key to clarity. For example, keeping certain project related notes in a dedicated folder without tagging them excessively helps you stay focused and avoids information clutter. At the same time, for overarching themes like personal growth or learning, you can rely on tags to make connections across different topics. It's all about balance. The final step then is to review and process the information regularly. Remember, it's not just about storing information. It's about transforming that information into actionable insights. Set aside time to process your notes. Ask yourself, how does this information help me move forward with my goals? Is there something actionable here? By doing this, you ensure that every piece of information has a purpose. So, to conquer information overload, remember these key points. Capture information from both our outer and inner world. Organize with a combination of folders and tags and regularly review your notes to turn them into actionable steps.
